Interpreting CTML Principles

Now that we have discussed how music may fit within the Dual-Coding model (See page: CTML Dual-Coding Theory) we can use the assumptions we have made to interpret the principles of CTML as they might apply to music instruction.

Mayer defines a set of 15 principles for Multimedia Learning in his book “Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning” (Mayer, 2014, p. 63), and describes how each of the techniques should be applied. Each principle is linked to one of the following goals: Minimising extraneous processing, managing essential processing, or fostering generative processing.


Minimising Extraneous Processing

Principle Description
Coherence Principle Eliminate extraneous materials
Signaling Principle Highlight essential materials
Redundancy Principle Don't add printed text to spoken text
Spatial Contiguity Principle Place printed text near corresponding graphic(s)
Temporal Contiguity Principle Present related narration and graphic simultaneously
Segmenting Principle Break the presentation into parts


Managing Essential Processing

Principle Description
Pre-Training Principle Describe names and key characteristics of elements before the lesson
Modality Principle Use spoken words rather than printed words
Multimedia Principle Use words and pictures together rather than words alone


Fostering Generative Processing

Principle Description
Personalisation Principle Put words in conversational style
Voice Principle Use human voice for spoken words
Embodiment Principle Give onscreen characters human-like gestures
Guided Discovery Principle Provide hints and feedback as learners problem-solve
Self-Explanation Principle Ask learners to explain a lesson to themselves
Drawing Principle Ask learners to make drawings for the lesson
